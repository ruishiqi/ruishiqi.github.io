Featured result

Dipolar Excitonic Insulators

Electron-hole bilayers provide a tunable platform where electrons and holes bind into interlayer excitons and organize into strongly correlated insulating states.

Research Question

Can a solid-state electron-hole system form an equilibrium excitonic insulator, and can its collective transport and thermodynamics be measured directly?

In van der Waals heterostructures, electrons and holes can be placed in separate but closely spaced layers. This geometry creates long-lived dipolar excitons whose density, imbalance, and interaction strength can be tuned electrically.

Key Result

By combining optical probes of local thermodynamics with transport measurements, this work established strongly correlated electron-hole bilayers as a platform for equilibrium excitonic insulators. The phase is identified through thermodynamic signatures of electron-hole pairing and through near-perfect Coulomb drag, where motion in one layer is locked to the opposite motion in the other.

The broader message is that interlayer excitons in two-dimensional semiconductors behave not simply as dilute optical quasiparticles, but as an interacting quantum fluid with measurable collective electronic response.
